Cabbage Soft Rot

Host Plants:

On Crops: Cabbage, cauliflower, lettuce, beans, peas

Where Found:

Cool temperate climates with abundant rainfall

Description:

Plants grow normally until a few days after a period of cool rain. First, outer leaves begin to wilt. On the undersides of the wilted patches, a soft cottony mold develops. At this point, plants emit a distinct rotting smell. A spell of warm weather with temperatures above 75°F (24°C) will accelerate the progress of this disease.

Damage:

The fungi invade the plant's circulatory system, causing it to rot from within. As the disease progresses, plants flop over and die. Infected plants are rendered inedible.

Preventing Problems:

Ensure plants receive good air circulation and ample sunlight, and keep weeds controlled to promote prompt drying after rains. Use mulch to prevent soil from splashing onto plants in heavy rains. Avoid using sprinklers or other overhead irrigation methods after cabbage, broccoli, cauliflower, or lettuce heads have formed. At the end of the season, pull up and compost plant material so it will be completely rotted by the following summer. In beds where this disease has been observed, grow grains and other non-susceptible crops for three years.

Managing Outbreaks:

Pull up affected plants, chop them into pieces, and compost them in an active compost pile. Do not turn under diseased plant material, as it may survive winter intact. Managed composting is needed to ensure that cabbage or broccoli stumps rot completely by the following summer.
